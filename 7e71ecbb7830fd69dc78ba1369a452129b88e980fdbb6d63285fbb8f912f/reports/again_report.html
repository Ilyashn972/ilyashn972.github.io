Delayed Recall (Again) — Narrative Report

This report summarizes work on delayed recall experiments: subjects recalled the Costa story immediately and again after a delay (one month for text-only, one week for multimedia). It covers methodology, key statistics (c and n), structural findings (fan-out, intrusions), a notable result on the “orange” detail, and the use of an LLM to split and map recall chunks to the narrative.

1. Experiments and methodology

Two experiments were run:

  • Experiment 1 (text-only): Subjects read the Costa story as text and wrote recall immediately, then again after one month. 32 users returned for the delayed recall.
  • Experiment 2 (multimedia): Subjects listened to an illustrated version (audio + images) and wrote recall immediately, then again after one week. 39 users returned for the delayed recall.

Intrusions were identified with a simple heuristic: fan_out == 0 (segments not mapped to any narrative clause in the recall).

3. Fan-out distribution and intrusions

The fan-out distribution was added to the report that compares immediate vs delayed recalls. Main observations:

  • fan_out == 0 grows significantly for delayed recalls (more “intrusion-like” or unmapped segments).
  • Higher fan-out values are more frequent in delayed recalls.

4. Orange recall finding (September 2025)

A concrete detail—the orange in the Costa story—was used to compare text-only vs multimedia and immediate vs delayed recall:

  • Text-only (no pictures): 7 of 44 subjects remembered the orange immediately; none after a month.
  • Multimedia: 18 of 42 subjects recalled the orange immediately; 10 still remembered it after a week (39 recalls in total). Only 8 subjects recalled the orange both immediately and after a week.

This illustrates how modality and delay affect recall of a specific visual detail.

5. LLM split-and-map and compression ratio (December 2025)

Recall-to-narrative mapping for delayed recalls is challenging: delayed recalls are more ambiguous, and segmenting the recall text itself was not fully reliable. To address this, an approach was tried where an LLM (GPT-5.2) is asked in a single call to both split the recall into chunks and map each chunk to contiguous narrative segments.

At first glance the results looked reasonably good. Example pages:

  • Costa (no summary) after a month — recall chunks to narrative chunks (GPT-5.2)
  • Costa multimedia after a week — recall chunks to narrative chunks (GPT-5.2)

The compression ratio (narrative length / recall length) was computed and plotted as a density (to account for shorter delayed recalls). Only subjects who provided both immediate and delayed recalls were included.
